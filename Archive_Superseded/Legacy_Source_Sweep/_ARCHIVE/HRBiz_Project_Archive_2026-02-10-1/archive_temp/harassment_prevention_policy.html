I. POLICY STATEMENT

Sapphos Environmental Inc. ("Sapphos" or "the Company") is committed to providing a workplace free from discrimination, harassment, and retaliation. Every employee, applicant, contractor, intern, and volunteer has the right to work in an environment where they are treated with dignity and respect.

Sapphos Environmental Inc. strictly prohibits all forms of unlawful discrimination, harassment (including sexual harassment), and retaliation.

This prohibition applies to all aspects of employment and covers conduct by employees, supervisors, managers, clients, vendors, contractors, and any other third parties with whom employees interact in connection with their work.

Violations of this policy will result in appropriate disciplinary action, up to and including termination of employment.

The Company encourages all employees to report any conduct that violates this policy. Retaliation against anyone who reports harassment, participates in an investigation, or opposes unlawful practices is strictly prohibited and will result in disciplinary action.

This policy is established in accordance with the California Fair Employment and Housing Act (Gov. Code § 12900 et seq.) and applicable federal law.

II. SCOPE AND COVERAGE

A. Who Is Protected

This policy protects:

  • All employees (full-time, part-time, temporary, seasonal, probationary)
  • Job applicants
  • Interns and trainees
  • Volunteers
  • Independent contractors and consultants
  • Former employees (for retaliation protections)

B. Where This Policy Applies

This policy applies to conduct occurring:

  • At Company premises (offices, field sites, laboratories, project locations)
  • At Company-related events (conferences, training sessions, business meetings, social functions, team-building activities)
  • During business travel (hotels, restaurants, transportation, client sites)
  • In virtual/remote work environments (video calls, email, instant messaging, collaboration platforms, company intranet)
  • Off-site when work-connected (client entertainment, professional networking events)
  • On social media when interactions are work-related or involve coworkers

C. Third-Party Conduct

Sapphos is committed to protecting employees from harassment by non-employees (clients, vendors, subcontractors, consultants, regulatory agency personnel) and prohibits harassment of non-employees by Sapphos employees.

Employees who experience harassment by third parties should report it immediately using the reporting procedures in Section V. The Company will take appropriate action, which may include discontinuing business relationships with third parties who engage in harassment.

III. PROTECTED CHARACTERISTICS

Sapphos prohibits discrimination, harassment, and retaliation based on the following protected characteristics (Gov. Code § 12940):

  • Race (including traits historically associated with race such as hair texture and protective hairstyles including braids, locks, and twists)
  • Color
  • National origin or ancestry
  • Citizenship status
  • Religion or creed (including religious dress and grooming practices)
  • Age (40 years and older)
  • Sex (including pregnancy, childbirth, breastfeeding, and medical conditions related to pregnancy or childbirth)
  • Gender, gender identity, and gender expression (including transgender status and transitioning)
  • Sexual orientation
  • Marital status (including registered domestic partnership status)
  • Military or veteran status
  • Disability (physical or mental, including HIV/AIDS status, cancer, genetic characteristics)
  • Medical condition (including genetic information, cancer, and a record or history of cancer)
  • Genetic information
  • Reproductive health decision-making (Gov. Code § 12945.8) – including decisions to use or not use any drug, device, or medical service related to reproductive health
  • Reproductive loss (Gov. Code § 12945.6) – including leave taken for pregnancy loss (miscarriage, stillbirth, unsuccessful IVF, failed adoption or surrogacy)
  • Off-duty cannabis use (Gov. Code § 12954) – lawful use of cannabis outside the workplace away from work premises during non-working hours
  • Criminal history (arrest or conviction records, participation in diversion programs, expunged or sealed records)

This policy also prohibits adverse action based on:

  • Requesting or taking protected leave (FMLA, CFRA, pregnancy disability leave, sick leave, reproductive loss leave, bereavement leave)
  • Filing a workers' compensation claim
  • Whistleblowing or reporting legal violations
  • Requesting reasonable accommodation for disability or religious practice
  • Engaging in protected union activity or concerted activity
  • Requesting lactation accommodation

IV. PROHIBITED CONDUCT

A. Discrimination

Discrimination is treating an individual or group differently in employment decisions based on a protected characteristic.

Prohibited discriminatory conduct includes:

  • Hiring or recruitment: Refusing to hire, recruit, or interview based on protected status
  • Compensation: Paying different wages or benefits based on protected status
  • Promotion or advancement: Denying promotion opportunities based on protected status
  • Job assignments: Assigning less desirable work, projects, or shifts based on protected status
  • Training and development: Excluding from training or professional development based on protected status
  • Discipline: Imposing harsher discipline based on protected status
  • Termination or layoff: Selecting for termination based on protected status rather than legitimate business criteria
  • Terms and conditions of employment: Providing different working conditions, benefits, or privileges based on protected status
Examples of discrimination:
  • Refusing to hire women for field work positions based on assumptions about physical capabilities
  • Paying employees of different races different salaries for substantially similar work
  • Denying promotion to an employee because of their age
  • Assigning less prestigious projects to employees based on national origin
  • Excluding an employee from client meetings because of their religious attire
  • Terminating an employee shortly after learning of their disability or medical condition
  • Denying job applicants based on criminal history without conducting individualized assessment

B. Harassment (Non-Sexual)

Harassment is unwelcome conduct based on a protected characteristic that is sufficiently severe or pervasive to create a hostile, intimidating, or offensive work environment, or that interferes with work performance.

A hostile work environment exists when harassment is severe or pervasive enough that a reasonable person would find the work environment intimidating, hostile, or abusive, and the victim subjectively perceives it as such.

Prohibited harassment includes:

Verbal Harassment:

  • Slurs, epithets, or derogatory comments based on protected characteristics
  • Offensive jokes, insults, or name-calling
  • Negative stereotyping or generalized statements about protected groups
  • Mocking accent, speech patterns, or language
  • Comments about physical appearance related to protected characteristics
  • Repeated unwelcome social invitations after clear rejection
  • Intrusive questions about protected characteristics (e.g., religious practices, medical conditions, immigration status, reproductive choices)

Visual Harassment:

  • Displaying or circulating offensive images, cartoons, posters, or graffiti
  • Sending offensive emails, text messages, or social media posts
  • Using offensive screen savers, backgrounds, or avatars
  • Making derogatory gestures

Physical Harassment:

  • Unwanted touching, patting, or brushing against someone
  • Assault or threats of physical harm
  • Blocking movement or cornering someone
  • Physical intimidation (standing too close, leaning over)
  • Damaging personal property based on protected status
Examples of non-sexual harassment:
  • Repeatedly telling jokes mocking a coworker's religion or religious practices
  • Circulating cartoons depicting racial stereotypes
  • Making repeated negative comments about older workers being "out of touch" or "behind the times"
  • Mocking a coworker's accent or asking "where are you really from?"
  • Making derogatory comments about an employee's pregnancy or suggesting they are less committed to work
  • Asking intrusive questions about an employee's disability or medical treatment
  • Commenting negatively on an employee's use of reproductive health services or reproductive loss leave
  • Excluding an employee from team activities because of their sexual orientation
  • Making negative comments about an employee's lawful off-duty cannabis use
  • Repeatedly asking about criminal history after hire or making derogatory comments about past convictions

C. Sexual Harassment

Sexual harassment is unwelcome conduct of a sexual nature. Under California law (Gov. Code § 12940(j)), sexual harassment includes two types of prohibited conduct:

1. Quid Pro Quo Sexual Harassment ("This for That")

Quid pro quo harassment occurs when submission to or rejection of sexual conduct is used as the basis for employment decisions affecting an individual.

Examples:
  • A supervisor tells an employee that a promotion depends on going on a date
  • A manager suggests that an employee's job security depends on tolerating sexual advances
  • A supervisor offers preferential treatment (better assignments, schedule, pay) in exchange for sexual favors
  • A supervisor retaliates against an employee who rejects sexual advances (poor performance review, demotion, termination, undesirable job assignment)
  • A supervisor conditions training opportunities on acceptance of sexual propositions
Key point: Even a SINGLE INCIDENT of quid pro quo harassment can violate this policy and the law.

2. Hostile Work Environment Sexual Harassment

Hostile environment sexual harassment occurs when unwelcome sexual conduct is sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of employment and create an abusive work environment.

Examples:

Verbal: Sexual comments about appearance, body, or clothing; sexual jokes or innuendos; sexually explicit stories; graphic descriptions of sexual activities; repeated requests for dates after clear rejection; making "kissing" or other sexual sounds; sexual nicknames or terms of endearment; intrusive questions about sexual life, preferences, or activities; discussing sexual conquests or experiences; rating attractiveness or sexual desirability

Visual: Displaying sexually suggestive posters, calendars, photographs, or screensavers; sending sexually explicit emails, texts, memes, or images; making sexual gestures; staring or leering at body parts; repeatedly looking someone up and down; displaying sexual content on personal devices visible to others

Physical: Unwanted touching (hugging, kissing, patting, rubbing, massaging); deliberately brushing against someone; blocking movement or cornering; sexual assault or attempted assault; grabbing or groping

Other: Following someone; waiting for someone; giving unwanted gifts of a romantic or sexual nature; love letters or notes after rejection; repeated attempts to establish romantic relationship after clear rejection

Virtual/Remote Environment Examples:
  • Sending sexually suggestive messages via Slack, Teams, email, or text
  • Making sexual comments during video calls about appearance, clothing, or home environment
  • Sharing sexually explicit content in group chats or channels
  • Making unwanted romantic advances via virtual communication
  • Commenting on body or appearance during video meetings
  • Using sexually suggestive virtual backgrounds or profile images
Important Note: Sexual harassment can occur between individuals of any gender or sexual orientation. It can be committed by supervisors, coworkers, clients, vendors, or any other person in connection with work.

D. Retaliation

Retaliation is any adverse action taken against an individual because they engaged in protected activity.

Protected Activities Include:

  • Reporting discrimination, harassment, or other violations (internally or to external agencies)
  • Filing a complaint with the California Civil Rights Department (CRD), Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC), or other government agency
  • Participating in an investigation (as complainant, witness, or investigator)
  • Opposing unlawful practices (objecting to discriminatory conduct, refusing to follow discriminatory orders)
  • Requesting reasonable accommodation for disability or religious practice
  • Taking protected leave (FMLA, CFRA, sick leave, pregnancy disability leave, reproductive loss leave)
  • Requesting lactation breaks or accommodation
  • Filing a workers' compensation claim
  • Whistleblowing (reporting violations of law, regulation, or public policy)
  • Engaging in union activity or protected concerted activity

Forms of Retaliation (Examples):

  • Termination, demotion, suspension, or other disciplinary action
  • Denial of promotion, training, or advancement opportunities
  • Negative or unjustified performance review
  • Undesirable job assignment, transfer, or schedule change
  • Reduction in hours, pay, or benefits
  • Increased scrutiny, micromanagement, or unrealistic performance standards
  • Exclusion from meetings, projects, or communications
  • Hostile treatment, shunning, or social isolation
  • Threats or intimidation
  • Spreading rumors or making false statements
  • Creating obstacles to performance (withholding resources, information, or support)
Critical Point: Retaliation is independently unlawful even if the underlying harassment or discrimination complaint is found to be unsubstantiated. Making a GOOD-FAITH COMPLAINT is protected, even if the investigation does not find a violation.

E. Inappropriate Conduct

Beyond conduct that violates the law, Sapphos discourages inappropriate workplace conduct that, while not necessarily severe or pervasive enough to constitute illegal harassment, is unprofessional and inconsistent with our values of respect and dignity.

Examples of inappropriate conduct:
  • Off-color jokes or comments (even if not directed at protected characteristic)
  • Persistent unwelcome social invitations after polite decline
  • Comments on physical appearance unrelated to work (even if not sexual)
  • Gossip or rumors about coworkers' personal lives
  • Exclusionary behavior or cliques that make coworkers feel unwelcome
  • Condescending or dismissive treatment
  • Yelling, cursing, or aggressive communication

Employees who experience or witness inappropriate conduct are encouraged to report it so the Company can address issues before they escalate to unlawful harassment. Reports of inappropriate conduct will be handled confidentially and addressed through coaching, counseling, or other appropriate measures.

VI. INVESTIGATION PROCEDURES

A. Company's Commitment to Prompt Investigation

Sapphos is committed to conducting a prompt, fair, thorough, and impartial investigation of all harassment, discrimination, and retaliation complaints.

Upon receiving a complaint, the Company will:

  1. Acknowledge receipt of the complaint to the complainant
  2. Assess severity and implement interim protective measures if necessary
  3. Assign a qualified investigator (internal or external)
  4. Conduct a thorough investigation
  5. Reach a determination based on the evidence
  6. Take appropriate remedial action if violations are found
  7. Notify the complainant and respondent of the outcome (in general terms)
  8. Monitor to ensure no retaliation occurs

B. Investigation Timeline

The Company will complete investigations within approximately [PLACEHOLDER: INVESTIGATION_TIMELINE] days of receiving a complaint, absent extenuating circumstances. If additional time is needed, the complainant will be notified.

C. Investigator Qualifications

Investigations will be conducted by qualified individuals with training in workplace investigations, including:

[PLACEHOLDER: INVESTIGATOR_NAME – e.g., External employment attorney, Licensed Private Investigator, HR Director with investigative training]

The Company may retain external investigators (attorneys or licensed private investigators) when appropriate to ensure impartiality and expertise.

D. Investigation Process

Phase 1: Intake and Protective Measures (Days 0-1)

  • Complaint received: The EEO Officer or other recipient documents the complaint
  • Initial assessment: Severity evaluated; immediate risks identified
  • Interim measures implemented if necessary:
    • Temporary separation of complainant and respondent (reassignment, schedule changes, interim leave)
    • No-contact directives
    • Other measures to ensure safety and prevent retaliation
  • Complainant notification: Complainant informed that investigation will proceed and that they are protected from retaliation

Phase 2: Investigation (Days 2-[TIMELINE])

The investigator will:

1. Gather Evidence

  • Emails, text messages, instant messages, social media posts
  • Documents, photographs, videos, audio recordings
  • Personnel files, performance reviews, attendance records
  • Any other relevant physical or digital evidence

2. Conduct Interviews

Complainant Interview:

  • Detailed account of alleged conduct
  • Specific incidents (dates, times, locations, witnesses)
  • Impact on work and well-being
  • Any prior complaints or attempts to resolve
  • Witnesses or evidence to corroborate allegations

Respondent Interview:

  • Notification of allegations (with sufficient detail to respond)
  • Opportunity to respond fully to each allegation
  • Respondent's account of events
  • Witnesses or evidence supporting respondent's version
  • Due process: right to be heard and present evidence

Witness Interviews:

  • Individuals identified by complainant or respondent
  • Others with relevant knowledge
  • Objective questioning to avoid bias
  • Documentation of witness statements

3. Site Visits (if applicable)

  • Visit location where conduct allegedly occurred
  • Assess visibility, accessibility, opportunity for conduct

4. Assess Credibility

The investigator will evaluate credibility using established factors:

  • Inherent plausibility: Is the account internally consistent and logical?
  • Corroboration: Is the account supported by witnesses, documents, or other evidence?
  • Motive to lie or bias: Does the individual have a reason to be untruthful or exaggerate?
  • Perception and opportunity: Could the person have reasonably perceived what they describe? Were they in a position to observe or experience the conduct?
  • Consistency: Are statements consistent over time and across interviews?
  • Demeanor: (Used cautiously) Does the person appear candid and forthcoming?

Phase 3: Findings and Determination

The investigator will reach a determination using the preponderance of the evidence standard: Is it more likely than not that the alleged conduct occurred?

Possible Findings:

  • Sustained: The investigation found sufficient evidence that the alleged conduct occurred and violated this policy
  • Not Sustained: The investigation found insufficient evidence to determine whether the conduct occurred (inconclusive)
  • Unfounded: The investigation found evidence that the alleged conduct did not occur

The determination will be documented in a written investigation report that includes:

  • Summary of complaint
  • Investigation steps taken
  • Evidence reviewed
  • Witnesses interviewed
  • Credibility assessments
  • Findings and conclusions
  • Recommended remedial action (if violations found)

Phase 4: Remedial Action

If the investigation sustains the complaint (finds violations), the Company will take appropriate corrective action, which may include:

Discipline of the Harasser:

  • Verbal or written warning
  • Mandatory training or counseling
  • Suspension (with or without pay)
  • Demotion or reassignment
  • Reduction in pay or benefits (if appropriate to circumstances)
  • Termination of employment

The specific discipline will be determined based on:

  • Severity and pervasiveness of the conduct
  • Whether conduct was physical or verbal
  • Position of the harasser (supervisors held to higher standard)
  • Prior complaints or violations
  • Impact on the victim
  • Other relevant circumstances

Other Corrective Measures:

  • Separation of parties (reassignment, schedule changes)
  • Policy updates or clarifications
  • Team or department-wide training
  • Enhanced monitoring or supervision
  • Restoration of benefits or opportunities denied due to harassment

If the investigation does not sustain the complaint but reveals other policy violations (e.g., unprofessional conduct, inappropriate behavior), the Company may still take corrective action.

Phase 5: Notification

Complainant Notification:

The complainant will be informed:

  • That the investigation is complete
  • Whether the complaint was sustained, not sustained, or unfounded (general outcome)
  • That appropriate action has been or will be taken (without specific details of discipline imposed on respondent, which is confidential)
  • That they are protected from retaliation and should report any retaliatory conduct immediately

Respondent Notification:

The respondent will be informed:

  • That the investigation is complete
  • The findings (whether allegations were sustained, not sustained, or unfounded)
  • Any disciplinary or corrective action being taken (respondent's own discipline)
  • That retaliation is prohibited

Note: Specific details of discipline imposed on the respondent are confidential and will not be shared with the complainant unless necessary for safety or other compelling reasons.

Phase 6: Post-Investigation Monitoring

Following the investigation, the Company will:

  • Monitor for retaliation: Check in with complainant to ensure no retaliatory conduct occurs
  • Follow up: Assess whether remedial measures are effective
  • Document: Maintain records of all follow-up actions
  • Respond promptly: If retaliation or additional issues arise, investigate immediately

E. Confidentiality

The Company will maintain confidentiality to the extent possible given its duty to investigate and comply with the law.

What this means:

  • Information about the complaint and investigation will be shared only on a "need-to-know" basis
  • Need-to-know individuals include: complainant, respondent, witnesses, investigators, HR, legal counsel, management (if necessary for remedial action)
  • Participants in the investigation (complainant, respondent, witnesses) will be instructed not to discuss the investigation with others to protect privacy and the integrity of the process
  • However: Complete confidentiality cannot be guaranteed because:
    • The Company must conduct a fair investigation, which requires interviewing the respondent and witnesses
    • The Company may have legal obligations to disclose information (e.g., in response to subpoena or government investigation)
    • The respondent has due process rights to know the allegations and respond

All investigation records (complaint, interview notes, evidence, investigative report) will be maintained in the Litigation/Investigations File, separate from personnel files, with access strictly limited to authorized personnel.

F. No Predetermined Outcome

The Company does not prejudge complaints. All investigations are conducted fairly and impartially. The investigator's role is to gather facts and reach an objective determination based on the evidence.

G. Good-Faith Participation

The Company expects all participants in an investigation (complainants, respondents, witnesses) to:

  • Participate in good faith
  • Provide truthful and complete information
  • Cooperate with the investigator
  • Maintain confidentiality as instructed

However: Making a complaint in good faith—even if the investigation does not substantiate it—is not misconduct and does not subject the complainant to discipline. Good-faith complaints are protected from retaliation.

Knowingly making a false complaint (deliberately lying or fabricating allegations) is a violation of this policy and may result in disciplinary action. However, the Company recognizes the difference between:

  • Unsubstantiated complaints (insufficient evidence to determine what occurred) – NOT subject to discipline
  • False complaints made in bad faith (deliberate lies with intent to harm) – MAY be subject to discipline
When in doubt, report. The Company encourages reporting of any conduct that may violate this policy. You will not be disciplined for making a good-faith report, even if the investigation does not find a violation.

IX. RETALIATION PROTECTIONS

A. Zero Tolerance for Retaliation

Sapphos Environmental Inc. strictly prohibits retaliation against any individual who:
  • Reports harassment, discrimination, or other policy violations (internally or externally)
  • Files a complaint with the California Civil Rights Department (CRD), Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC), or other government agency
  • Participates in an investigation as a complainant, witness, or investigator
  • Opposes unlawful or unethical conduct
  • Requests reasonable accommodation for disability or religious practice
  • Takes protected leave (FMLA, CFRA, pregnancy disability leave, sick leave, reproductive loss leave, etc.)
  • Engages in other protected activity under California or federal law

B. What Constitutes Retaliation

Retaliation is any adverse action taken because an individual engaged in protected activity.

Adverse actions include, but are not limited to:

  • Termination, layoff, or constructive discharge (forcing someone to resign)
  • Demotion, transfer to less desirable position, or reassignment
  • Suspension or disciplinary action
  • Denial of promotion, raise, or advancement opportunity
  • Negative or unjustified performance evaluation
  • Exclusion from meetings, projects, training, or communications
  • Reduction in hours, pay, or benefits
  • Undesirable shift or schedule changes
  • Increased scrutiny, micromanagement, or unrealistic performance standards
  • Hostile treatment, shunning, or social isolation by coworkers or management
  • Spreading rumors or making defamatory statements
  • Threats or intimidation
  • Creating obstacles to job performance (withholding information, resources, or support)

Retaliation can be subtle. Even actions that seem minor (being excluded from a lunch meeting, receiving a suddenly critical email) can constitute retaliation if they are motivated by the individual's protected activity and would dissuade a reasonable person from engaging in protected activity.

C. Retaliation Is Independently Unlawful

Retaliation is a separate violation from harassment or discrimination. Even if an investigation finds that the underlying harassment complaint was not substantiated, retaliation against the complainant is still unlawful and prohibited.

Making a complaint in good faith protects you from retaliation, even if the investigation does not find a violation.

D. Reporting Retaliation

If you believe you have been retaliated against, report it immediately using the same reporting procedures outlined in Section V of this policy.

The Company will investigate retaliation claims promptly and will take immediate corrective action if retaliation is found.

E. Consequences for Retaliation

Employees who engage in retaliation will be subject to disciplinary action up to and including termination of employment.

All harassment, discrimination, and retaliation complaints and investigations are documented and maintained in accordance with California law.

  • Written or documented complaints
  • Interview notes and witness statements
  • Evidence (emails, documents, photos, etc.)
  • Investigation reports and findings
  • Disciplinary or corrective action documentation
  • Follow-up and monitoring records

Storage Location:

All investigation records are stored in the Litigation/Investigations File, which is maintained separately from general personnel files.

Access Restrictions:

Access to investigation records is strictly limited to:

  • EEO Officer
  • HR Director / Interim HR Manager
  • Legal Counsel
  • Authorized investigators
  • Others on a need-to-know basis as approved by the EEO Officer or Legal Counsel

Retention Period:

Investigation records are retained for [PLACEHOLDER: RETENTION_PERIOD – recommend minimum 3 years from complaint date; consult legal counsel for specific retention requirements and litigation holds].

B. Training Records

Training records are maintained for each employee and include:

  • Training completion date
  • Type of training (supervisory or non-supervisory)
  • Training provider
  • Proof of completion (certificate, roster signature, or electronic confirmation)

Storage Location:

Training records are maintained in the [Training File / Personnel File].

Retention Period:

Training records are retained for a minimum of 2 years from the date of training (Gov. Code § 12950.1).

C. Confidentiality of Records

All records related to harassment, discrimination, and retaliation complaints are maintained confidentially to the extent permitted by law. Unauthorized disclosure of investigation records is prohibited and may result in disciplinary action.
