## 2. SB 553 - WORKPLACE VIOLENCE PREVENTION

### Status: ACTIVE - Effective [EFFECTIVE DATE] (Ongoing Compliance Required)

**What It Is:**
Comprehensive workplace violence prevention plan requirement with training, incident tracking, and annual review.

**Who Must Comply:**
Almost all California employers (exemptions: <10 employees at all times, law enforcement, some healthcare already covered)

**Key Requirements:**

#### Written Violence Prevention Plan (WVPP)
Must include:
1. **Responsible persons:** Name, title, contact info
2. **Workplace evaluation:** Hazard assessment, risk factors
3. **Hazard identification:** Process for identifying threats
4. **Incident investigation:** How to investigate and document
5. **Hazard correction:** Timeline and methods for fixes
6. **Training procedures:** Initial + annual training plan
7. **Incident reporting:** How employees report threats
8. **Emergency response:** Run-Hide-Fight + domestic violence protocols

#### Types of Violence Covered:
- **Type I:** Criminal intent (robbery, trespassing)
- **Type II:** Customer/client aggression
- **Type III:** Worker-on-worker (bullying, fights)
- **Type IV:** Personal relationships (domestic violence in workplace)

#### Training Requirements
- **Initial training:** All employees upon hire (1+ hours)
- **Annual training:** Every year thereafter
- **Additional training when:**
  - New hazards identified
  - New prevention methods implemented
  - Employee requests training
  - Cal/OSHA requires it

**Training Must Cover:**
1. Copy of company WVPP
2. Recognizing warning signs
3. Reporting procedures
4. Response protocols (Run-Hide-Fight)
5. De-escalation techniques
6. Use of alarm systems
7. How to seek assistance

#### Incident Log Requirements
Must record within 24 hours:
- Date, time, location
- Employee name and job title
- Detailed incident description
- Type classification (I, II, III, or IV)
- Investigation findings
- Corrective actions taken

**Retention:** 5 years

#### Annual Review
Review and update plan:
- At least annually
- After workplace violence incident
- When new hazards identified
- Document all changes made

**Cal/OSHA Enforcement:**
- Serious violation: [AMOUNT]-[AMOUNT]
- No written plan: [AMOUNT]-[AMOUNT]
- Inadequate training: [AMOUNT]-[AMOUNT]

## 4. SB 616 - PAID SICK LEAVE EXPANSION

### Status: ACTIVE - Effective [EFFECTIVE DATE] (Ongoing Compliance)

**What It Is:**
Expansion of California's paid sick leave from 3 days/24 hours to 5 days/40 hours annually.

**Who Must Comply:**
ALL California employers (including part-time, temporary, seasonal)

**Key Changes:**

#### Accrual Requirements
**Option 1: Accrual Method**
- Employees accrue 1 hour of PSL per 30 hours worked
- Minimum carry-over: 40 hours to next year
- Employers can cap accrual at 80 hours

**Option 2: Lump Sum Front-Loading**
- Provide 40 hours (5 days) at start of year
- No accrual tracking needed
- Must provide full amount to new hires OR allow accrual

#### Usage Requirements
- Employees can use minimum 40 hours per year
- Can limit usage to 40 hours per year (even if more accrued)
- Must allow use after 90 days of employment
- Can set reasonable minimum increments (e.g., 2-hour blocks)

#### Permissible Uses:
1. Employee's own illness/injury/medical care
2. Preventive care
3. Diagnosis/care/treatment for existing condition
4. Care for family member (same reasons)
5. Domestic violence, sexual assault, or stalking (victim services, legal, medical)

**Family Member Defined:**
- Child, parent, spouse, registered domestic partner
- Grandparent, grandchild, sibling
- Designated person (1 person per year)

#### Pay Requirements:
- Pay at employee's regular rate
- Must appear on wage statement
- Pay on regularly scheduled payday

#### Notice Requirements:
**To Employees:**
- Written notice of PSL rights at hire
- Amount available on wage statements OR separate notice
- Post Labor Commissioner's workplace poster

**From Employees:**
- Can require reasonable advance notice (when foreseeable)
- Cannot require doctor's note for <3 days
- Can request documentation for >3 consecutive days

#### Record Keeping:
- Maintain records for 3 years:
  - Hours worked
  - PSL accrued
  - PSL used
  - PSL carried over

- [AMOUNT] per employee per day (underpayment)
- [AMOUNT] per employee per day (retaliation)

## 5. AB 2188 - CANNABIS EMPLOYMENT PROTECTIONS

### Status: ACTIVE - Effective [EFFECTIVE DATE] (Ongoing Compliance)

**What It Is:**
Protects employees/applicants from discrimination based on off-duty cannabis use (with exceptions).

**Who Must Comply:**
ALL California employers (with specific exemptions)

**Key Provisions:**

#### Prohibited Actions:
Employers CANNOT:
1. Discriminate in hiring based on off-duty cannabis use
2. Discriminate in employment based on off-duty cannabis use
3. Use drug tests that detect non-psychoactive cannabis metabolites
4. Request information about off-duty cannabis use (unless allowed)

#### What This Means:
- **Drug tests must detect THC** (psychoactive), not metabolites
- **Cannot reject applicant** solely for positive test showing past use
- **Cannot discipline employee** for off-duty use
- **Must use impairment-based testing** if testing for cannabis

#### Major Exemptions:
Protections DO NOT apply to:
1. **Building & construction trades** (safety-sensitive)
2. **Federal contractors/licensees** (federal law conflicts)
3. **Positions requiring federal background checks**
4. **When employer would lose federal contract/funding**
5. **When federal law preempts state law**

#### Drug Testing Considerations:

**Allowed:**
- Testing for actual impairment (e.g., saliva test for recent use)
- Zero-tolerance policies for on-duty use
- Testing after workplace accident
- Reasonable suspicion testing (observable impairment)
- DOT/federally mandated testing

**Prohibited:**
- Urine tests showing metabolites (past use, not impairment)
- Hair testing for cannabis
- Blanket refusal to hire based on positive test
- Discipline for off-duty use without impairment

#### Best Practices:

**Update Drug Testing Policies:**
1. Review current testing methods
2. Switch to impairment-based testing for cannabis
3. Maintain zero-tolerance for on-duty use
4. Keep testing for other drugs unchanged
5. Document federal exemptions if applicable

**Update Job Postings/Applications:**
1. Remove "drug-free workplace" language (or clarify)
2. Add language about on-duty impairment prohibition
3. Note federal exemptions if applicable

**Train Managers:**
1. Recognizing on-duty impairment vs. off-duty use
2. Documentation requirements
3. Reasonable suspicion protocols
4. How to handle positive tests

**Penalties:**
- FEHA discrimination claims
- PAGA violations
- Labor Commissioner complaints
- Potential damages + attorney fees

**Industries Most Affected:**
- Tech companies (many have dropped cannabis testing)
- Retail and hospitality
- Professional services
- Healthcare (non-federal roles)

**Industries Less Affected:**
- Construction (exempted)
- Federal contractors (exempted)
- Transportation (DOT rules apply)

## 6. AB 1076 - CRIMINAL RECORD RELIEF & FAIR CHANCE ACT

### Status: ACTIVE - Ongoing Compliance

**What It Is:**
Automatic criminal record relief + "ban the box" requirements for hiring.

**Who Must Comply:**
Employers with 5+ employees (criminal history inquiry restrictions)

**Key Requirements:**

#### "Ban the Box" Basics:
**Cannot ask about criminal history:**
- On job applications
- Before conditional job offer
- In initial interview

**Can only ask:**
- AFTER making conditional offer
- Before final hiring decision

#### Criminal History Inquiry Process:

**Step 1: Job Posting & Application**
- Remove questions about criminal history
- Cannot ask verbally in interviews
- Cannot conduct background check yet

**Step 2: Interview & Selection**
- Evaluate qualifications without criminal history
- Make hiring decision based on job requirements

**Step 3: Conditional Offer**
- Extend offer contingent on background check
- NOW can ask about/check criminal history

**Step 4: Background Check Review**
- Can only consider convictions (not arrests)
- Must conduct individualized assessment
- Cannot have blanket ban on felonies

#### Individualized Assessment Required:

If criminal history found, must consider:
1. **Nature and gravity of offense**
2. **Time elapsed** since conviction/completion of sentence
3. **Nature of job** being sought
4. **Evidence of rehabilitation**

**Cannot automatically disqualify** for:
- Arrests without conviction
- Infractions
- Sealed/expunged records
- Juvenile records
- Cannabis offenses now legal

#### Pre-Adverse Action Requirements:

If deciding not to hire based on criminal history:

**Step 1: Written Notice**
Must provide:
- Copy of background check report
- Copy of Fair Chance Act notice (from DFEH)
- Explanation of why history disqualifies
- Right to respond (5 business days)

**Step 2: Wait Period**
- Allow 5 business days for response
- Must consider any evidence provided
- Document consideration process

**Step 3: Final Decision**
If still deciding not to hire:
- Provide written notice of final decision
- Explanation of decision
- Copy of conviction history used
- Right to file discrimination complaint

#### LA City Fair Chance Initiative for Hiring (FCIHO):

**Additional Requirements in LA:**
- Ban the box (similar to state law)
- Covers employers with 10+ employees
- [AMOUNT]-[AMOUNT] per violation fine
- Additional disclosure requirements

#### Exceptions:

Can consider criminal history without restrictions for:
1. Positions where law requires background check
2. Positions with vulnerable populations (children, elderly, disabled)
3. Law enforcement positions
4. Specific statutory disqualifications (e.g., healthcare licensing)

**Best Practices:**

1. **Remove from applications:**
   - "Have you ever been convicted of a crime?"
   - Checkbox for criminal history
   - Essay questions about criminal past

2. **Update hiring process:**
   - Train hiring managers on Fair Chance Act
   - Create individualized assessment template
   - Document all hiring decisions
   - Use consistent background check timing

3. **Create assessment criteria:**
   - List job-specific safety/security requirements
   - Define acceptable vs. disqualifying offenses
   - Document business necessity
   - Consider time elapsed

**Penalties:**
- DFEH discrimination complaint
- [AMOUNT]-[AMOUNT] per violation (LA FCIHO)
- Damages + attorney fees
- Reputational harm

**Free Resources:**
- Job application template (Fair Chance compliant)
- Individualized assessment worksheet
- Pre-adverse action notice template
- Final adverse action notice template

## 8. SB 699 - BEREAVEMENT LEAVE

### Status: ACTIVE - Effective [EFFECTIVE DATE] (Ongoing Compliance)

**What It Is:**
Unpaid bereavement leave for death of family member.

**Who Must Comply:**
Employers with 5+ employees

**Key Requirements:**

#### Leave Entitlement:
- **5 days** of unpaid leave
- **Per qualifying event** (each family member death)
- **No annual cap** (can use multiple times per year)
- **Consecutive or intermittent** use allowed

#### Qualifying Family Members:
- Spouse or domestic partner
- Child (biological, adopted, foster, stepchild, legal ward)
- Parent (biological, adoptive, foster, step, in-law)
- Sibling
- Grandparent
- Grandchild

#### Usage Requirements:

**When can be taken:**
- Within 3 months of death
- Can extend if needed (discuss with employer)

**How it can be taken:**
- All 5 days at once
- Intermittently (1 day here, 2 days there)
- Based on employee need

**Advance notice:**
- Not required (bereavement is sudden)
- Should notify employer as soon as practicable
- Can request documentation (death certificate, obituary)

#### Relationship to Other Leave:

**Does NOT count against:**
- CFRA leave
- FMLA leave  
- Pregnancy disability leave
- Other protected leave

**Can run concurrent with:**
- Paid sick leave (if available)
- Vacation/PTO (if employee chooses)
- Other employer-provided bereavement

**Employer can:**
- Require use of accrued PTO for pay
- Require use of sick leave for pay
- Provide additional paid bereavement days

#### Documentation:

**Employer can request:**
- Death certificate
- Obituary or published notice
- Verification of family relationship

**Cannot require:**
- Excessive documentation
- Information about cause of death
- Details about funeral arrangements (unless relevant to leave timing)

#### Job Protection:
- Employee returns to same position OR
- Equivalent position with same pay/benefits
- No retaliation for taking leave
- Benefits continue during leave

**Best Practices:**

1. **Update leave policies:**
   - Add bereavement leave section
   - Clarify paid vs. unpaid
   - Define family members
   - Explain request process

2. **Train managers:**
   - Handle requests compassionately
   - Don't ask intrusive questions
   - Process leave promptly
   - Document properly

3. **Coordinate with other leave:**
   - Explain options to employee
   - Allow paid leave if desired
   - Track leave usage separately

4. **Communicate clearly:**
   - Provide written confirmation
   - Explain return-to-work process
   - Maintain confidentiality

**Penalties:**
- DFEH complaint
- Private lawsuit
- Damages + attorney fees
- PAGA violations

## 9. AB 2499 - WORKERS' COMPENSATION PRESUMPTIONS (WAREHOUSE/DISTRIBUTION)

### Status: ACTIVE - Effective [EFFECTIVE DATE]

**What It Is:**
Creates rebuttable presumption that certain warehouse injuries are work-related.

**Who Must Comply:**
Employers operating **warehouse distribution centers** with:
- 100+ non-managerial employees at a single facility OR
- 1,000+ non-managerial employees at one or more facilities

**Key Provisions:**

#### Covered Injuries:
If employee works at covered warehouse AND develops:
1. **Musculoskeletal injuries**
2. **Repetitive motion injuries**
3. **Cardiovascular disease** (heart/blood vessels)

**Presumption:** Injury is presumed work-related and compensable

#### What "Presumption" Means:

**For Employee:**
- Don't have to prove injury is work-related
- Burden shifts to employer to disprove
- Easier to obtain workers' comp benefits

**For Employer:**
- Must prove injury is NOT work-related to deny claim
- More claims likely approved
- Higher workers' comp costs expected

#### Rebutting the Presumption:

Employer can overcome presumption by showing:
- Injury occurred outside work
- Employee has pre-existing condition
- Injury not related to work activities
- Other substantial evidence

**Burden is on EMPLOYER to prove**

#### Practical Impacts:

**For Warehouse Employers:**
1. **Expect more claims:** Presumption makes filing easier
2. **Higher premiums:** More approved claims = higher costs
3. **Need prevention:** Focus on ergonomics/safety
4. **Better documentation:** Track non-work injuries/activities

**For Employees:**
1. Easier to obtain benefits
2. Less documentation required initially
3. Protections for work-related conditions

#### Prevention Strategies:

1. **Ergonomic assessments:**
   - Review lifting procedures
   - Assess workstation setup
   - Rotate tasks to reduce repetition
   - Provide assistive equipment

2. **Safety training:**
   - Proper lifting techniques
   - Stretching/warm-up programs
   - Break reminders
   - Reporting procedures

3. **Health programs:**
   - Pre-shift stretching
   - Wellness initiatives
   - Cardiovascular screening (with consent)
   - Return-to-work programs

4. **Documentation:**
   - Track employee health history (compliant)
   - Document all safety training
   - Maintain incident logs
   - Investigate all claims thoroughly

**Industries Most Affected:**
- Amazon, Target, Walmart warehouses
- Third-party logistics (3PL) providers
- Distribution centers
- Large fulfillment facilities

- Warehouse safety checklist
- Ergonomic assessment template
- Workers' comp claim response protocol

## 11. ONGOING FEDERAL REQUIREMENTS

### Federal Laws That Still Apply

#### A. FAIR LABOR STANDARDS ACT (FLSA)

**Key Requirements:**
- Federal minimum wage ([AMOUNT] - but CA/LA higher)
- Overtime pay (1.5x for 40+ hours/week)
- Child labor protections
- Record keeping (3 years)

**Exempt vs. Non-Exempt:**
- Executive, administrative, professional exemptions
- Computer professional exemption
- Outside sales exemption
- Salary basis test ([AMOUNT]/week in CA for 2026)

#### B. FAMILY AND MEDICAL LEAVE ACT (FMLA)

**Eligibility:**
- Employers with 50+ employees
- Employees worked 1,250 hours in past 12 months
- Within 75 miles of worksite with 50+ employees

**Leave Entitlement:**
- 12 weeks unpaid per year
- Job protection
- Health benefits continuation

**Qualifying Reasons:**
- Birth/adoption/foster care
- Employee's serious health condition
- Family member's serious health condition
- Military family leave

**Interaction with CFRA:**
- Run concurrently when possible
- CFRA broader in some ways (no 50-employee proximity rule)
- Federal form required (WH-380)

#### C. AMERICANS WITH DISABILITIES ACT (ADA)

**Requirements:**
- Reasonable accommodation for disabilities
- Interactive process
- Cannot discriminate
- Applies to 15+ employees

**Reasonable Accommodations:**
- Modified work schedule
- Reassignment to vacant position
- Equipment/devices
- Leave of absence

**Undue Hardship Defense:**
- Significant difficulty or expense
- Based on employer's resources
- Case-by-case analysis

#### D. TITLE VII (FEDERAL DISCRIMINATION)

**Protected Classes:**
- Race, color, religion, sex, national origin
- Broader than FEHA in some ways
- Applies to 15+ employees

**Religious Accommodations:**
- Must accommodate unless undue hardship
- Different standard than ADA
- Includes dress, grooming, schedule

#### E. IMMIGRATION REQUIREMENTS

**I-9 Verification:**
- Required for ALL employees
- Within 3 days of hire
- Reverify if authorization expires
- Cannot discriminate based on citizenship status

**E-Verify (if required):**
- Federal contractors must use
- Some states require (not CA)
- Voluntary in California

**Common Violations:**
- Accepting wrong documents
- Failing to reverify
- Keeping I-9s with personnel files (should be separate)
- Not completing in time

#### F. CONSOLIDATED OMNIBUS BUDGET RECONCILIATION ACT (COBRA)

**Requirements:**
- Employers with 20+ employees
- Offer continued health coverage after qualifying event
- Up to 18-36 months depending on event

**Qualifying Events:**
- Termination (non-misconduct)
- Reduction in hours
- Employee death
- Divorce/separation
- Dependent aging out

**Cal-COBRA:**
- California's version for 2-19 employees
- Up to 36 months coverage
- Similar to federal COBRA
